NK Fitness Landscapes

Stuart Kauffman's NK model is a dial for ruggedness. Each of N genes contributes to fitness depending on its own state and K others — so K is how much each gene's effect depends on the rest (its epistasis). At K = 0 the landscape is smooth: nearby genotypes have similar fitness, so hill-climbing always finds the top — like the weasel. Crank K up and it dissolves into noise, a mass of local optima — deceptive traps, like HIFF. Drag the slider and watch it happen.

Each tiny square is one genotype, laid out so that neighbouring squares differ by a single mutation (both axes run in Gray-code order). Colour is fitness. At K = 0 you get smooth patches — a real hill you can climb — because a gene's effect doesn't depend on the others. Raise K and the picture breaks into static: a local optimum (a square fitter than all its neighbours) appears almost everywhere, and a hill-climber landing on the global peak (gold) goes from a sure thing to a long shot.

number of local optima (log) chance hill-climbing finds the global peak

Sweeping K from 0 to N−1: local optima multiply (roughly 2N/(N+1) at maximum epistasis), while the odds of a hill-climber reaching the global optimum collapse toward zero. This is Kauffman's complexity catastrophe — beyond a point, more interdependence between genes makes good solutions undiscoverable by local search. It's the quantitative reason recombination and other global operators matter, and the bridge from the weasel to HIFF.
